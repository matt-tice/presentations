* Frobenius Algebras

** Definitions

A /(generalized) Frobenius algebra/ $(F, \gen{\cdot, \cdot})$, is a not-necessarily associative nor unital algebra $F$, with non-degenerate bilinear form $\gen{\cdot, \cdot}: F \otimes F \to k$, such that
\[
\gen{xy,z} = \gen{x,y z}
\]
for all $x,y,z \in F$.

=Classical case:= Restricts to finite-dimensional algebras
- Can define them in terms of compatible algebraic-coalgebraic structure.

=Integrals:= We think of Frobenius algebras as generalizing algebras of integrable functions

* Hopf Algebras

** Definition

A Hopf algebra is a unital associative $k$-algebra, $H$, with maps 

\medskip

- \fbox{Comultiplication: } $\Delta: H \to H \otimes H$

- \fbox{Counit: } $\varepsilon: H \to k$

- \fbox{Antipode: } An involution $S: H \to H$

\bigskip

satisfying the appropriate compatibility conditions between the algebraic and coalgebraic structure.

| UE algs.    | $U[\mathfrak{g}]$           | $\Delta p = 1 \otimes p + p \otimes1$ | $\varepsilon(p) = 0$ |

* Hopf-Frobenius Modules

A left /Hopf-Frobenius module/ is a pair $(H,F)$ where $H$ is a Hopf algebra, $F$ is a Frobenius algebra and $F$ is a left $H$-module, such that the following conditions are satisfied:

\bigskip

- \fbox{Weak Cartan: }  $\sum \gen{g_{(1)} \cdot x, g_{(2)} \cdot y} = \varepsilon(g) \gen{x,y}$

- \fbox{Antipode Condition: }  $\gen{S(g) \cdot x, y} = \gen{x, g \cdot y}$

* Exterior Derivatives and Connections

** Definitions

Let $F_{1}, F_{2}$ be Frobenius algebras, $\{e_i\}_{i \in I}$ be a basis for $F_2$, and let $\{ \partial_i : F_{1} \to F_1 \}_{i \in I}$ be a collection of derivations on $F_1$ indexed by the basis of $F_2$. 
The  /exterior derivative/ of $f$ with respect to the basis $\{ e_i \}$ and derivations $\{\partial_i \}$ is the map 
$$d: F_1 \to F_1 \otimes F_{2}$$
defined by
\[
df = \sum_i \partial_i f \otimes e_i
\]
for all f \in F_1.

=Intuition:= $F_1$ is a Frobenius algebra of functions, and $F_2$ is a vector space encoding direction. The algebra $F_1 \otimes F_2$ plays the role of Kähler differentials $\Omega^1(F_1)$

* Connections and Directional Derivatives

** Definition
Let $F_{1}$ be a fixed Frobenius algebra. 
Given a Frobenius algebra $F_3$, an $F_1$-/connection on $F_3$/ is a choice of Frobenius algebra $F_{2}$ and linear morphism 
\[
\nabla: F_{3} \to F_1 \otimes F_2 \otimes F_3.
\]

\bigskip

When $F_1$ and $F_3$ are clear, we will simply refer to $\nabla$ as the connection.

** Definition

Let $\nabla: F_3 \to F_1 \otimes F_2 \otimes F_3$ be a connection, and $d: F_1 \to F_1 \otimes F_2$ be an exterior derivative. The /covariant derivative with respect to $\nabla$/ is the map $D_\nabla: F_1 \otimes F_3 \to F_1 \otimes F_2 \otimes F_3$ defined by
\[
D_{\nabla} (f \otimes\theta)= df \otimes \theta + (f \otimes 1 \otimes 1) \nabla\theta
\]


* Physics Examples

We consider the Frobenius algebras 

\bigskip

=Functions:= $F_f = \mathcal{S}(\mathbb{R}^4)$

\bigskip

=Spacetime:= $F_{ST} = (\mathbb{R}^{4}, \eta)$

\bigskip

=Gauge:= $F_G = (V, \gen{\cdot, \cdot})$


* Connections on Spacetime

A /connection on spacetime/ has the form
\begin{align*}
\nabla: F_{ST} \to F_f \otimes F_{ST} \otimes F_{ST}.
\end{align*}
If we take $F_{ST} = \mathbb{R}^4$,
\[
\nabla e_j = \sum_{ik} \Gamma_{ij}^k(x) \otimes e_i \otimes e_k.
\]
The functions $\Gamma_{ij}^k(x)$ are the /Christoffel symbols/.


=Covariant derivative:= 
\begin{align*}
D_\nabla f &= df + \sum_i f_i \otimes \nabla e_i \\
&= \sum_{ij} \partial_j f_i \otimes e_j \otimes e_i + \sum_{ijk} f_i(x) \Gamma_{ji}^k (x) \otimes e_j \otimes e_k.
\end{align*}

* Connections on Spacetime

Apply the bilinear form to the second tensor factor:

=Directional derivative:= 
\begin{equation*}
\gen{v,D_\nabla f}_2 = \sum_{ij} v_j \partial_j f_i \otimes e_i + \sum_{ijk} \Gamma^k_{ji} v_j f_i \otimes e_k,
\end{equation*}

\bigskip

The covariant and directional derivatives match those from differential geometry.


* Gauge Connections

A /gauge connection/ has the form
\begin{align*}
\nabla: F_{G} \to F_f \otimes F_{ST} \otimes F_{G}.
\end{align*}

Let $\{ e_i \}$ be a basis for $F_{ST}$ and $\{ \theta_j \}$ be a basis for $F_G$.

=Gauge Potential:= If we expand out in a basis, we can write
\[
\nabla \theta_j = \sum_{ik} A_{ij}^k(x) \otimes e_i \otimes \theta_k.
\]
$A_{ij}^k(x)$ are the \textbf{gauge field} of the gauge connection

=Covariant derivative:= 
\begin{align*}
D_\nabla f 
&= \sum_{ij} \partial_j f_i \otimes e_j \otimes \theta_i + \sum_{ijk} f_i(x)  A_{ij}^k(x) \otimes e_j \otimes \theta_k.
\end{align*}

* Gauge Connections

=Directional derivative:= 
\begin{align*}
\gen{v,D_\nabla f }_2
&= \sum_{ij} v_j \partial_j f_i \otimes \theta_i + \sum_{ijk} v_j f_i(x)  A_{ij}^k(x) \otimes \theta_k.
\end{align*}
